# Thermal Pulse

## Algorithmic Philosophy

There exists a rhythm beneath the data — a planetary heartbeat encoded in a century of temperature readings. "Thermal Pulse" is a generative movement that treats climate anomaly data not as information to be charted, but as vital signs to be felt. Each temperature reading becomes a breath: a contraction and expansion of thousands of particles arranged into an implied sphere, their collective motion tracing the fever of a living world. The algorithm does not illustrate data; it metabolizes it, transforming numbers into organic tension that accumulates on the canvas like sediment in geological time.

The computational foundation is a dual-layer flow field system of meticulous construction. The primary field is a spherical noise topology — concentric rings of Perlin noise that define the planet's "skin," where particles are born, travel, and expire. The secondary field is driven by the actual NASA GISS annual temperature anomaly values (1880–2024), which modulate the noise amplitude, turbulence frequency, and displacement magnitude of the primary field. In cool anomaly years, the field is laminar: particles flow in long, graceful arcs with minimal deviation, their trails thin and translucent, the sphere appearing to breathe slowly and deeply. As anomaly values rise, the noise octaves multiply, the displacement grows violent, and particles are torn from their smooth orbits into chaotic spirals — the sphere exhales in short, ragged bursts. This is a meticulously crafted system where every parameter ratio was calibrated through painstaking iteration by a master of computational aesthetics.

Color is not decoration but thermodynamics made visible. The palette transitions along a carefully designed gradient anchored to real temperature deltas: deep glacial indigo (#0B0B2B) and cerulean (#1B4F72) for the pre-industrial baseline, shifting through steel-blue to warm amber (#D4A03C) at the inflection point, and finally into searing vermillion (#C0392B) and incandescent white-orange (#F39C12) at the extremes. Particles carry their birth-year's color as an identity, so the accumulated canvas becomes a stratigraphic record — cooler tones layered underneath, hotter tones burning on top. The color mapping was refined through countless iterations until each hue felt thermodynamically inevitable, not aesthetically arbitrary. This is the product of deep expertise in chromatic calibration.

The compositional logic follows the geometry of a breathing body. Particles are seeded along concentric ellipses that imply a sphere seen from above — dense at the equator, sparse at the poles. Their radial displacement creates the "breathing" effect: in calm years, particles barely deviate from their orbital paths; in volatile years, they scatter outward like expelled air, leaving gaps and voids in the sphere's surface that reveal the black vacuum beneath. The accumulation of tens of thousands of particle trails over 144 years of data creates a density map that is simultaneously a portrait of the planet and a medical chart of its condition. Every stroke placement emerges from the algorithm's execution, yet the final composition feels as if a master printmaker labored over each line with painstaking attention to weight, spacing, and atmospheric depth.

The temporal architecture compresses a century and a half into a single image through layered rendering. Early years (low anomaly) are drawn first with high transparency and fine strokes, forming a ghostly baseline. Later years overprint with increasing opacity and stroke weight, so the present literally burns through the past. The result is not a graph with an axis — it is a palimpsest where time is depth, temperature is texture, and the viewer reads the planet's deteriorating health not through numbers but through the visceral shift from order to turbulence, from breath to gasp. Each seed produces a unique composition, yet every variation carries the same unmistakable signature: the quiet early layers giving way to the violent present. This is master-level implementation — an algorithm that appears simple in concept but required deep computational expertise to achieve the precise balance between data fidelity and aesthetic transcendence.
